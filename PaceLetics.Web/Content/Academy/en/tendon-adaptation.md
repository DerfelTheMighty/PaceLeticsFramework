Tendons connect muscles and bones. But they are not passive structures. They are adaptable biological tissue that transmits force and stores energy.

The majority of a tendon consists of extracellular matrix, primarily collagen type I. This collagen is organized hierarchically: molecules form fibrils, fibrils form fibers, fibers form fiber bundles and this creates the entire tendon. Between these structures lie water, proteoglycans, glycoproteins and other matrix components. Together they determine how the tendon transmits tensile forces, stores and releases elastic energy. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

The most important cells in this tissue are tenocytes. They lie elongated between the collagen fibers and maintain the matrix. Compared to muscle, the cell density of the tendon is quite low. This is important for understanding tendon adaptation: The largest portion of the tissue is not cell mass, but rather long-lasting matrix. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[13]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1560025/full)

## Why adaptation takes time

Adaptation processes occur much more slowly in tendons than in muscles. Muscle tissue is better supplied with oxygen and nutrients and also responds to training through rapid neuronal adaptations. Tendon tissue is metabolically rather sluggish. The core of adult Achilles tendons shows very low collagen turnover based on carbon-14 data. This does not mean that tendons are immutable. However, it does mean that structural adjustments are more likely to occur over periods of weeks to months rather than days. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x) [[15]](https://faseb.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1096/fj.12-225599)

Kubo et al. show this time course very clearly: After isometric plantar flexion training, markers for collagen synthesis, collagen content and collagen structure changed after around two months; a significant increase in Achilles tendon stiffness was evident after three months. The opposite direction is also interesting: After detraining, part of the mechanical adaptation was lost relatively quickly. Tendon adaptation is therefore not only slow to develop, but also load-dependent in its maintenance. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x)

When people talk about a “stiffer” tendon in training, that is not meant in a negative way. Higher tendon stiffness means that the tendon lengthens less for the same force. This can be advantageous for sporting exercise: force is transmitted more efficiently, elastic energy can be used better and the relative stretch of the collagen framework remains lower at high forces. It becomes problematic when muscle strength increases faster than the resilience of the associated tendon. The mechanical stress on the tendon tissue then increases, although the athlete subjectively feels more efficient. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9)

This divergence between muscle and tendon development is particularly relevant for young athletes. Mersmann, Bohm and Arampatzis describe that muscles and tendons do not necessarily react synchronously to growth, training and maturation. Plyometric and sport-specific training can quickly improve performance and muscle strength, while tendons need more specific stimuli. This picture also fits from running injury research: beginner runners have a significantly higher incidence of injury per 1000 hours of running than more experienced recreational runners. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[7]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-015-0333-8)

## How voltage becomes a signal

For a tendon to adapt structurally, mechanical load must be translated into a cellular response. Two levels can be distinguished. It is crucial that tenocytes perceive mechanical load themselves. When the tendon is stretched, the tension in the matrix changes, fluid moves in the tissue, cell processes are deformed and forces are transmitted via contact points between matrix and cell. This cellular mechanosensing is the starting point for matrix remodeling. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

The translation of mechanical stress into a biological cell response is called **mechanotransduction**. An important mechanism involves connecting proteins between the extracellular matrix and the tenocyte cytoskeleton. When the tendon is stressed, these structures register the tensile stress and transmit it as a signal to the interior of the cell. Mechanosensitive ion channels and other mechanosensitive structures also contribute to the development of a biological response from a mechanical stimulus. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[13]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1560025/full)

Inside the cell, these signals influence which genes are activated and how the cell interacts with its environment. During physiological stress, tenocytes promote collagen synthesis, organize the extracellular matrix, and maintain tissue homeostasis. On the other hand, if the stress is permanently too high, too low or otherwise unphysiological, the cell response shifts increasingly towards matrix degradation and inflammation-related remodeling processes. The tendon does not simply react to **more training**, but above all to **how** it is loaded. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

Cyclic loading can also influence mechanical properties at the level of the collagen network itself. Susilo et al. showed in an experimental model that cyclic tensile loading can strengthen a collagen network without necessarily involving active cells. This does not replace cell biology for human training, but it shows that the matrix is ​​not just a passive receiver, but part of the mechanical adaptation. [[6]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsfs.2015.0088)

## Which training stimuli work?

From a training perspective, this results in an important principle: Too low a load does not provide sufficient stimulus; very high or poorly dosed load can promote microtrauma and maladaptive matrix changes. What makes sense is a mechanical zone in which tenocytes repeatedly experience high but controllable tensile stress. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9) [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html)

Achilles tendon intervention studies fit this logic well. Bohm et al. examined how strain rate and strain duration influence adaptation. A high tendon elongation, low frequency protocol with approximately 3 seconds of loading plus 3 seconds of unloading for 14 weeks significantly increased tendon stiffness, cross-sectional area and Young's Modulus. Longer holding times also worked, but were not automatically superior. Very rapid jump loads less clearly led to a global increase in stiffness. [[1]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/217/22/4010/13248/Human-Achilles-tendon-plasticity-in-response-to)

Mersmann et al. formulate a specific tendon protocol for young athletes: 5 sets with 4 contractions, 85 to 90 percent of maximum isometric strength, with around 3 seconds of high tendon tension per contraction. The point is not as much volume as possible, but rather a clear mechanical stimulus with sufficient intensity and controlled duration. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full)

Meta-analyses support this focus. Bohm, Mersmann and Arampatzis and Lazarczuk et al. report that mechanical loading can improve tendon stiffness, material properties and, in some cases, the cross-section. The local tendon stretch or the load intensity seems to be particularly relevant. The specific form of muscle action is probably less important than the question of what tension actually reaches the tendon. [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9) [[9]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01695-y)

## Conclusion

Tendons adapt slowly because they consist primarily of long-lasting extracellular matrix and are remodeled by comparatively few cells. These cells can sense mechanical tension and translate it into collagen synthesis, matrix organization, and material adaptation via mechanotransduction. This requires repeated, sufficiently high and controlled tension over weeks to months. Good tendon training is therefore less spectacular than consistent: high tension, good dosage, enough time and progression in the right order. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/) [[15]](https://faseb.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1096/fj.12-225599)
