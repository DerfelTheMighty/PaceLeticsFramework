Tendons connect muscles and bones. But they are not passive structures. They are an adaptable biological tissue that transmits force and stores energy.

The majority of a tendon consists of extracellular matrix, primarily collagen type I. This collagen is organized hierarchically: molecules form fibrils, fibrils form fibers, fibers form fiber bundles, and these structures form the whole tendon. Between them lie water, proteoglycans, glycoproteins, and other matrix components. Together they determine how the tendon transmits tensile forces, stores elastic energy, and releases it again. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

The most important cells in this tissue are tenocytes. They lie elongated between the collagen fibers and maintain the matrix. Tendon stem/progenitor cells are also discussed as possible contributors to repair and remodeling processes. Compared with muscle, tendon cell density is low. This is central to understanding tendon adaptation: the dominant part of the tissue is not contractile cell mass, but long-lived matrix. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[13]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1560025/full)

## Why adaptation takes time

Adaptation processes occur much more slowly in tendons than in muscles. Muscle tissue is better vascularized, has high protein-turnover dynamics, and also responds to training through rapid neural adaptations. Tendon tissue is metabolically slower. Carbon-14 data show very little collagen turnover especially in the core of adult Achilles tendons. This does not mean that tendons are unchangeable. It does mean, however, that measurable structural adaptations usually emerge over weeks to months rather than days. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x) [[15]](https://faseb.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1096/fj.12-225599)

Kubo and colleagues show this time course very clearly: After isometric plantar flexion training, markers for collagen synthesis, collagen content and collagen structure changed after about two months; a significant increase in Achilles tendon stiffness was evident after three months. The opposite direction is also interesting: After detraining, part of the mechanical adaptation was lost relatively quickly. Tendon adaptation is therefore not only slow to develop, but also load-dependent in its maintenance. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x)

When people talk about a “stiffer” tendon in training, that is not meant in a negative way. Higher tendon stiffness means that the tendon lengthens less for the same force. This can be advantageous for sporting exercise: force is transmitted more efficiently, elastic energy can be used better and the relative stretch of the collagen framework remains lower at high forces. It becomes problematic when muscle strength increases faster than the resilience of the associated tendon. The mechanical stress on the tendon tissue then increases, although the athlete subjectively feels more efficient. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9)

This divergence between muscle and tendon development is particularly relevant for young athletes. Mersmann, Bohm and Arampatzis describe that muscles and tendons do not necessarily react synchronously to growth, training and maturation. Plyometric and sport-specific training can improve performance and muscle strength relatively quickly, while tendons require more specific, high-tension stimuli. This picture also fits from running injury research: beginner runners have a significantly higher incidence of injury per 1000 hours of running than more experienced recreational runners. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[7]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-015-0333-8)

## How tension becomes a signal

For a tendon to adapt structurally, mechanical load must be translated into a cellular response. Two levels can be distinguished.

The first level is neurophysiological. Golgi tendon organs are located at the muscle-tendon junction and register tension in the muscle-tendon system. If they are mechanically deformed, Ib afferents transmit information to the spinal cord. This sensory system is important for force regulation, proprioception and protective mechanisms. Other sensory structures such as free nerve endings and Ruffini and Pacini-like receptors may also play a role depending on the region. These receptors report mechanical information to the nervous system. [[14]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-021-21880-3)

The second level is cellular. For structural adaptation, it is crucial that tenocytes sense mechanical load themselves. When the tendon is stretched, the tension in the matrix changes, fluid moves in the tissue, cell processes are deformed and forces are transmitted via contact points between matrix and cell. This cellular mechanosensing is the starting point for matrix remodeling. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

The translation of mechanical load into a biochemical cellular response is called mechanotransduction. One central mechanism runs through integrins, the connecting proteins between extracellular matrix and cytoskeleton. Mechanical tension can influence integrin clustering, focal adhesions, and cytoskeletal tension. Mechanosensitive ion channels, calcium signals, primary cilia, and other mechanosensitive structures are also discussed. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[13]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/bioengineering-and-biotechnology/articles/10.3389/fbioe.2025.1560025/full)

Inside the cell, these inputs become signaling pathways that regulate gene expression and matrix metabolism. Relevant pathways include FAK, MAPK/ERK, PI3K/Akt/mTOR, TGF-beta/SMAD, IGF-1, and YAP/TAZ. They influence whether tenocytes synthesize collagen type I, organize matrix, regulate remodeling enzymes, or shift toward catabolic and inflammation-adjacent programs under unfavorable load. The tendon does not simply respond to "more training", but to the quality of the mechanical signal. [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/)

Cyclic loading can also influence mechanical properties at the level of the collagen network itself. In an experimental model, Susilo and colleagues showed that cyclic tensile loading can strengthen a collagen network without necessarily involving active cells. This does not replace cell biology for human training, but it shows that the matrix is ​​not just a passive receiver, but part of the mechanical adaptation. [[6]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsfs.2015.0088)

## Which training stimuli work?

From a training science perspective, this results in an important principle: Too low a load does not provide sufficient stimulus; very high or poorly dosed load can promote microtrauma and maladaptive matrix changes. What makes sense is a mechanical zone in which tenocytes repeatedly experience high but controllable tensile stress. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9) [[11]](https://www.cellphysiolbiochem.com/Articles/000743/index.html)

The Achilles tendon intervention studies fit this logic well. Bohm and colleagues examined how strain rate and strain duration influence adaptation. A high tendon elongation, low frequency protocol with approximately 3 seconds of loading plus 3 seconds of unloading for 14 weeks significantly increased tendon stiffness, cross-sectional area, and Young's modulus. Longer holding times also worked, but were not automatically superior. Very rapid jump loads less clearly led to a global increase in stiffness. [[1]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/217/22/4010/13248/Human-Achilles-tendon-plasticity-in-response-to)

Mersmann and colleagues formulate a specific tendon protocol for young athletes: 5 sets with 4 contractions, 85 to 90 percent of the maximum isometric force, with around 3 seconds of high tendon tension per contraction. The point is not as much volume as possible, but rather a clear mechanical stimulus with sufficient intensity and controlled duration. [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full)

Meta-analyses support this focus. Bohm, Mersmann and Arampatzis as well as Lazarczuk and colleagues report that mechanical loading can improve tendon stiffness, material properties and, in some cases, cross-section. The local tendon stretch or the load intensity seems to be particularly relevant. The specific form of muscle action - isometric, concentric or eccentric - is probably less important than the question of what tension actually occurs in the tendon. [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9) [[9]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01695-y)

Nevertheless, different forms of training have different practical roles.

Isometric loading is particularly suitable when tension needs to be precisely dosed or when painful tendons first have to be made tolerable to the load again. It generates high force with little joint movement and without high impact load. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885) [[10]](https://www.cell.com/heliyon/fulltext/S2405-8440(24)15181-7)

Heavy Slow Resistance combines high load with controlled movement and relatively long time under tension. For tendinopathies, especially of the patellar tendon, current literature suggests that progressive tendon loading and heavy slow resistance training should not be considered inferior to eccentric training alone. [[9]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01695-y) [[10]](https://www.cell.com/heliyon/fulltext/S2405-8440(24)15181-7)

Eccentric training continues to be relevant and historically well established. However, its effect is probably less because eccentricity as such has a special status, but rather because eccentric exercises allow high tendon forces and good progressive control. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9)

Explosive training and plyometrics are essential for sports ability, but they are not always the first step to tendon adaptation. Massey and colleagues showed that short explosive contractions can increase the stiffness of the free patellar tendon. However, for muscle hypertrophy, aponeurosis and the entire muscle-tendon complex, prolonged high tension was superior. In practical terms, this means: First build capacity and resilience, then integrate speed, reactivity and sport-specific spring function. [[3]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2018.01170/full)

This order is crucial for the return to sport. When it comes to Achilles tendon problems, Silbernagel and Crossley emphasize that return to sport is not decided by a single test. Pain progression, function, resilience, sport requirements and the reaction to progression are relevant. Pain is not a perfect tissue marker, but it is a useful monitoring signal. If symptoms increase significantly during the session or persist the next day, the stress was likely too high for the current level of adaptation. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885)

## Practical training framework

Tendon training should be planned over at least 12 weeks. The main stimulus is high, controlled tensile load. Depending on total load, 2 to 4 targeted tendon sessions per week are plausible. In practice, this often means intensities from around 70 percent 1RM or, for isometric work, 85 to 90 percent of maximum isometric force. What matters is that the tendon is actually loaded highly for several seconds. [[1]](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/217/22/4010/13248/Human-Achilles-tendon-plasticity-in-response-to) [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[8]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9)

The scope does not have to be extremely high. A useful framework is, for example, 4 to 5 sets of 4 to 8 repetitions or 5 sets of 4 heavy isometric contractions. The progression should first develop load and control, later adding speed, jumps, running volume and sport-specific load. [[3]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2018.01170/full) [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2017.00987/full) [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885)

A practical phase logic is:

1. Establish stress tolerance: isometric holding work, reduced running or jumping volume, symptom monitoring.
2. Build capacity: Heavy Slow Resistance, high tension, slow repetitions, enough recovery.
3. Training elastic function: moderate plyometrics, running ABC, hops, jumps, short accelerations.
4. Bring sport back: increase the load so that the intensity, volume, surface, fatigue and competitiveness do not all increase at the same time. [[5]](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2015.5885) [[10]](https://www.cell.com/heliyon/fulltext/S2405-8440(24)15181-7)

Tendons adapt slowly because they consist primarily of long-lasting extracellular matrix and are remodeled by comparatively few cells. These cells can sense mechanical tension and translate it into collagen synthesis, matrix organization, and material adaptation via mechanotransduction. This requires repeated, sufficiently high and controlled tension over weeks to months. Good tendon training is therefore less spectacular than consistent: high tension, good dosage, enough time and progression in the right order. [[2]](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-011-2248-x) [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11301520/) [[15]](https://faseb.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1096/fj.12-225599)
