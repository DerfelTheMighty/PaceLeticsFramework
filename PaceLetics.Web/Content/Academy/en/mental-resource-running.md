Running does not only train muscles and circulation. For many people it becomes a controllable moment in the day: a tool that steadies mood, lowers tension, and makes self-efficacy tangible. Current research suggests that running can be a low-threshold mental resource. A scoping review on running and mental health found that both single running bouts and regular running are associated with better mood, lower tension, and positive mental health outcomes. [[1]](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph17218059) Single bouts can help with self-regulation during acute tension, while regular running is associated with better mood and well-being. A systematic review of recreational running adds that motivation, self-regulation, habit, and perceived competence are central psychological factors for staying with running. [[2]](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.624783) Broader reviews support this interpretation: physical activity reduces depressive symptoms, anxiety, and psychological distress, and walking/jogging is among the well-studied forms of movement for depressive symptoms in a BMJ network meta-analysis. [[3]](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2022-106195) [[4]](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj-2023-075847) A recent Cochrane review also concludes that exercise can moderately reduce depressive symptoms compared with control conditions, while long-term evidence remains limited. [[5]](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD004366.pub7) Prospective cohort data also show that even moderate activity volumes are associated with a lower risk of depression. [[6]](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2022.0609) We do not want to inflate running into therapy or a therapy substitute. The point is that running can be a mental resource and that many people intuitively use it as one.

## Running as a social activity

Group activities can make running a more stable mental resource because they combine movement with social connection. Social connection, support, and a sense of belonging are considered protective factors for mental health, especially for depressive symptoms and anxiety. [[15]](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0275004) A recent best-evidence synthesis on physical activity and mental health also shows that social support and social connections are important mediating factors between exercise and mental health. [[16]](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-024-01676-6) This matters in practice for running groups: they create commitment, shared routines, and low-threshold belonging. Studies of beginners in running groups report improvements in mental well-being, self-efficacy, and running identity. [[17]](https://doi.org/10.1080/08964289.2022.2100865)

## Resource conservation as a training goal

If running is understood as a mental resource, then training must preserve this resource first. Performance development remains important: it shows adaptation, creates competence and can strengthen motivation. But maximum performance improvement is only the actual goal for a small proportion of athletes. For many, it is more valuable that running remains available: reliable, low-injury, suitable for everyday use and dosed in such a way that it strengthens rather than depletes self-efficacy.

One model for resource-based stress management is conservation of resources theory. [[13]](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-orgpsych-032117-104640) A key idea is that resources must not only be built, but also protected from loss; resource loss can itself become a stressor. In sport, self-regulation is therefore not only discipline or training toughness, but also the ability to perceive one's own state, actively steer recovery, and adjust load so that well-being, health, and long-term performance are preserved. [[14]](https://doi.org/10.1177/1747954119897528) For running training, this means that not every possible performance gain is automatically a desirable training stimulus. A good stimulus is one that makes the resource of running more likely: more resilience, more trust, more continuity.

This is particularly relevant for beginners and health-oriented runners. Studies of running programs show that a relevant proportion of participants drop out, with pain or injury among the central reasons. [[11]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2018.06.003) [[12]](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph20176682) Qualitative work also shows that runners experience injuries not only as a physical problem, but as an interruption of freedom, routine, and control. [[9]](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjsem-2021-001117) That is why the primary goal of running training should not be maximum adaptation at all costs, but preserving and expanding the resource. Performance development remains desirable, but it serves this resource, not the other way around.

## PaceLetics

PaceLetics starts exactly there: we want to understand sport as a resource. To preserve and expand this resource, we dose load, make routines understandable, protect long-term progress, and enable athletes to make their own decisions.
